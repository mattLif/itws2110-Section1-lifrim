Article I

Section 1: Investing Powers to the Legislative Branch

"All legislative Powers herein granted shall be vested in a Congress of the United States, which shall consist of a Senate and House of Representatives."

Analysis

In this section the legislative body is introduced. It is composed of a Senate and House of Representatives, which will have the powers to introduce and write the laws of the United States.

Section 2: Composition of the House of Representatives

"The House of Representatives shall chuse their Speaker and other Officers; and shall have the sole Power of Impeachment."

Analysis

The House of Representatives is formally introduced. It declares a state's population is tied to its number of representatives, how a state's population will be counted, and how this population will be evaluated every 10 years. Concepts of term limits and the power of Impeachment are also introduced, which will set the House apart from the Senate. It alone has the unique power of Impeachment.

Section 3: Composition of the Senate

"The Vice President of the United States shall be President of the Senate, but shall have no Vote, unless they be equally divided."

Analysis

The members, rules, and powers of the Senate are formally defined. Members must at least 35 years of age, 9 years a citizen, and 2 years a resident of their running state. If selected, they are 1 of 2 senators for their state, and will preside for a 6 year term. Their specific given power is to ratify an Impeachment vote from the House of Representatives. If a tie in the Senate ever occurs, then the Vice President is allowed to cast a tiebreaking vote.

Section 4: Gathering the Congress

"The Congress shall assemble at least once in every Year, and such Meeting shall be on the first Monday in December, unless they shall by Law appoint a different Day."

Analysis

Congress must meet at least once every year, on a date chosen by the members. It also gives the powers to the states to choose when and where they elect their representatives for either legislative body.

Section 5: Elections and Proceedings

"Each House may determine the Rules of its Proceedings, punish its Members for disorderly Behaviour, and, with the Concurrence of two thirds, expel a Member."

Analysis

Each House follows similar rules and is afforded similar freedoms in attending Congress and running their elections. A majority is required to be in attendance for Congress to be in session. Each is responsible for writing and following their own procedure for meetings, and are required to publish notes on their meetings in the Journal of Procedings. Neither House may adjorn for more than 3 days when Congress is in session.

Section 6: Compensation of Representatives

"The Senators and Representatives shall receive a Compensation for their Services, to be ascertained by Law, and paid out of the Treasury of the United States."

Analysis

Members are payed by the US Treasury, and are protected from arrest barring special circumstances: Treason, and Breach of the Peace. Additionally, while serving, a member may not hold another civil office in the United States.

Section 7: Writing Bills into Laws

"All Bills for raising Revenue shall originate in the House of Representatives; but the Senate may propose or concur with Amendments as on other Bills."

Analysis

This legislative body can write laws through Bills. After a bill is approved by both the House of Representatives and the Senate, it is delivered to the President for consideration. Upon the President's approval the Bill becomes law. If the Bill is rejected, or is left unaddressed for 10 days, the Congress may still sign the Bill into law through a 2/3rds majority in each House.

Section 8: Other Powers of Congress

"–And To make all Laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into Execution the foregoing Powers, and all other Powers vested by this Constitution in the Government of the United States, or in any Department or Officer thereof."

Analysis

This Section outlines many different powers of Congress. These include but are not limited to: production of currencies, securing financial institutions, establishing the Post Office, raise armies, and declare war.

Section 9: Limitations of Congress

"The Privilege of the Writ of Habeas Corpus shall not be suspended, unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it."

Analysis

In this section, various rules are outlined that limit the powers of Congress. An example of this is in the first paragraph, which prevents Congress from stopping the Slave Trade. This shows how the Founding Fathers considered both powers and limitations as constructs for building a benevolent government.

Section 10: Limitations of State Rights

"No State shall, without the Consent of Congress, lay any Duty of Tonnage, keep Troops, or Ships of War in time of Peace, enter into any Agreement or Compact with another State, or with a foreign Power, or engage in War, unless actually invaded, or in such imminent Danger as will not admit of delay."

Analysis

State rights are limited similarly to how the Congress limits itself in Section 9. These limitations extend the Congressional limitations to the state level, but also introduce some ability for States to self-govern.

Article II

Section 1: Electing a President

"The Congress may determine the Time of chusing the Electors, and the Day on which they shall give their Votes; which Day shall be the same throughout the United States."

Analysis

The process of choosing a president is introduced. First an Electoral College is established, and each elector votes for 2 candidates. Ballots are counted by the President of the Senate, and the winner (possibly chosen through a tie-breaker in the House of Representatives) is president. Candidates must be at least 35 and a citizen of at least 14 years.

Section 2: Powers of the President

"The President shall have Power to fill up all Vacancies that may happen during the Recess of the Senate, by granting Commissions which shall expire at the End of their next Session."

Analysis

A president has several powers to aid their execution of the office. First, he is the Commander in Chief, controls the US Armed Forces, and can grant pardons. Second, he can make treaties, appoint ambassadors, and appoint interim Senate Reccess members. However, the former two must be approved by the Senate.

Section 3: State of the Union

"He shall from time to time give to the Congress Information of the State of the Union..."

Analysis

A president is responsible for the State of the Union address, which informs the populace of the happenings of government. During this address, the president gives recommendations and considerations about the laws of Congress. The president may also receive ambassadors during this event.

Section 4: Removal from Office

"The President, Vice President and all civil Officers of the United States, shall be removed from Office on Impeachment for, and Conviction of, Treason, Bribery, or other high Crimes and Misdemeanors."

Analysis

A president and other officers of the US Government can be removed from office for the following charges: Treason, Bribery, high crimes and misdemeanors, or through impeachment proceedings.

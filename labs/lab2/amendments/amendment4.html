Amendment IV

"The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized."

Analysis

The Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution protects people from unreasonable searches and seizures by the government, balancing public safety with an individual's right to private propery. It requires that law enforcement obtain a warrant, supported by probable cause, before searching someone's home, property, or personal belongings. Similar to the Third Amendment, this reinforces the idea that an individual reserves the right to privacy unless a lawful reason is given. While the amendment itself refers to physical property ("houses, papers, and effects"), today it also includes digital data, such as phones and computers.
